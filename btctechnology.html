Digital cash is not a new phenomenon. It is estimated that more than 90% of world currencies are digital.

Cryptography isn't new either. A vast amount of modern security, such as banking and web protection, depends on cryptographic hash functions. Its roots go back to Julius Caesar and Egyptian Hieroglyphics

Cryptocurrencies, therefore, are digital currencies which are secured by cryptographic technologies.


What makes Bitcoin different?
Bitcoin is a peer-to-peer network in which transactions are publicly announced and the participants agree on the single order in which they were received.

The solution that Bitcoin offers is to verify whichever ledger has the most computational work put into it (a cryptographic hash function).

What keeps the consensus of this so-called ledger is something that is called a blockchain. Blocks are edits to the list, done by digital signatures. Digital signatures (done via a secret key) cannot be forged. And to make sure that there is a standard order to these blocks, a block has to contain the hash of the previous block.

If you use computational work as a basis for what to trust, you can make it so fraudulent transactions and conflicting ledgers would require an infeasible amount of computation.

This computational work is called Proof of Work (POW).

It is a process by which miners get to solve complex mathematical problems, and the winners are selected to add the newest batch of transactions to the blockchain.

Miners use GPU/CPU power. And their reward in BTC gets halved every four years.

POW also solves the Double-Spend problem.


In short, Bitcoin is a decentralized, trust-less verification system based on math born in cryptography.

There is no need for 3rd party mediators, like banks.

And each person on earth holds the potential to be their own bank.
